## The Core Distinction

The six beats in NARRATIVE.md are an architecture narrative disguised as a product story:
- Beat 1 (platform-cycle thesis) = architecture argument
- Beat 4 (context compounds) = architecture property
- Beat 5 (moat = accumulated context) = architecture defensibility

This works for VCs (they evaluate architectural bets). It doesn't work for users (they evaluate what they can hire).

YARNNN needs **two narrative modes** — not because the story is inconsistent, but because two audiences need two entry points into the same truth. The agent-native commitment (doc 09) sharpens both: the architecture narrative gains a revenue proof object, and the service model narrative gains the "hire a team" framing that resolves capability ambiguity.

## Narrative Mode B: The Service Model Narrative (Users, Landing Page, ProductHunt)

This is the user-facing narrative. It describes what you hire, not how it works.

### Beat 1: The Production Gap

"You have expertise worth packaging into products — newsletters, reports, data packages. You know the niche. You know the audience. But running the business alone means you're the researcher, the writer, the analyst, the designer, the delivery manager, and the business analyst. Every week. That's not a content product business. That's a second full-time job."

**Why "hire a team" resonates here**: The user's real problem isn't "I need AI to write for me" (that's ChatGPT). It's "I need a production department, a delivery department, and a business operations department." Framing the problem as a team gap — not a tool gap — sets up the solution.

### Beat 2: The Proof

**Before reference implementation**: "Nat Eliason runs a content operation generating $300K/year with autonomous agents. But his system is bespoke — custom-built, technical, not available to anyone else."

**After reference implementation**: "We run [product name] on YARNNN. [X] customers, [Y] MRR, spending 30 minutes a week on editorial review. Here's month 1 output vs. month 6. The team got better — and the revenue proves it."

**Showcase strategy (from doc 09)**: Lovable showcases apps. YARNNN should showcase **revenue and operational leverage**. "This person earns $X/month from their content product business, spending 30 minutes per week on editorial review." Revenue + time leverage is the proof object.

### Beat 3: Hire the Team

"Sign up and meet your team. Six roles, ready to work:

- **Researcher** tracks your sources and surfaces signals
- **Analyst** synthesizes patterns and connections
- **Writer** drafts content in your editorial voice
- **Tracker** monitors your customers, revenue, and competitive landscape
- **Designer** produces charts, data exports, and rendered assets
- **Thinking Partner** coordinates the team, recommends strategy, manages the business

Tell your team what niche you're in and what products you want to create. Connect Lemon Squeezy for commerce. Review the first draft. Approve. Put the checkout link in your bio."

**Key difference from architecture narrative**: No workspace, no filesystem, no recursive perception. The user sees: brief the team → they produce → you review and approve → customers pay → revenue grows. The product is a team you hired, not a platform you configured.

**The "hire" language is load-bearing.** "Hire a team" creates different expectations than "use a platform." You hire a team and expect them to work independently, improve over time, and handle operations you don't want to do. You use a platform and expect to do the work yourself with better tools. The metaphor shapes the entire user relationship.

### Beat 4: The Team Gets Better

"Your month 3 output is noticeably better than month 1 — and your editorial review is faster because the team learned your standards.

The Researcher's entity tracking is more precise. The Analyst surfaces patterns from 3 months of accumulated signals. The Writer needs fewer corrections because feedback distillation converged on your voice. The Tracker has 3 months of customer data powering real trend analysis. The Thinking Partner recommends strategy grounded in accumulated business intelligence.

Nobody told the team to improve. They improve the way any team improves — by doing the work, reading what came before, and getting better at the specific job."

**Why "team gets better" beats "AI writes better"**: People believe teams improve with tenure. Nobody disputes that a 6-month team is better than a fresh hire. "The AI writes better" sounds like marketing. "The team gets better at its job" sounds like common sense. Same underlying mechanism (recursive perception substrate), radically different reception.

### Beat 5: Why Switching Hurts

"Your subscriber list is portable. Your revenue stream is portable. Your content archive is exportable. But the team's accumulated intelligence is not.

Switch to ChatGPT + Beehiiv and your next issue starts from zero context. The research is generic. The style is default. The analytics are empty. Quality drops. Customers notice. Revenue declines.

The team you hired spent 6 months learning your niche, your standards, and your audience. That learning doesn't transfer."

**Key difference**: No "moat" or "switching costs" language (VC-coded). Instead: a visceral picture of what switching feels like. The user understands the moat as lived experience, not strategic framework.

### Beat 6: Get Started

"Brief your team today. Review the first draft. Put the checkout link in your bio. First month free — if your customers grow, the team is working."

**Two aha moments** (from doc 09):

- **Aha #1 (instant, acquisition)**: "I briefed the team and they produced a first draft with a checkout link in under 10 minutes." Target: sign-up to first product draft in under 10 minutes.
- **Aha #2 (delayed, retention)**: "My month 3 output is noticeably better than month 1, and my editorial review is faster." Lead with aha #1 at launch. Lead with aha #2 in the "3 months later" build-in-public update.

## The Two Narratives Side by Side

| Beat | Architecture Narrative (VCs) | Service Model Narrative (Users) |
|------|-----------------------------|---------------------------------|
| 1 | Platform-cycle thesis — application layer for content businesses | You need a team, not another tool |
| 2 | ClawdBot 17K stars + Felix $300K + reference implementation | Reference implementation: $X MRR on 30 min/week |
| 3 | Agent framework + workspace + recursive perception | Hire the team: 6 roles, brief them, review output |
| 4 | Team intelligence compounds via recursive perception substrate | The team gets better at its job — research, production, analytics |
| 5 | Architectural moat + unoccupied competitive position | Switch and feel the quality drop. Revenue declines. |
| 6 | SAM + seed round + timing + traction | Brief the team. First draft in 10 minutes. Link in bio. |

These aren't contradictory. They're the same truth told to two audiences. The architecture narrative explains *why* the team gets better. The service model narrative shows *what* you can hire.

3. **Update Beat 6 ICP.** Content product operator replaces solo consultant as the entry wedge. SAM needs resizing (creator economy market).

4. **Add Felix as Beat 2 proof point** alongside ClawdBot. Two proof vectors: demand for persistent AI (ClawdBot) + demand for autonomous business operations (Felix).

5. **Vocabulary updates:**

| Always say | Instead of | Reasoning |
|------------|-----------|-----------|
| "Hire a team" | "Use a platform" | Sets expectations: they work independently, improve over time |
| "Your team gets better at its job" | "Accumulated context compounds" | Matches human intuition, not architecture jargon |
| "Brief your team" | "Configure your workspace" | Hiring language, not setup language |
| "Review and approve" | "Supervise" | Editorial director posture, not quality gate |
| "Content product business" | "Information business" | User language, not VC language |
| "30 minutes a week" | "Operational leverage" | Concrete time claim, not abstract benefit |

6. **Add anti-pattern: "Platform with AI" framing.** Per doc 09, explicitly reject positioning that describes YARNNN as "Beehiiv but with AI" or "a content platform that uses AI." This triggers the feature parity comparison YARNNN loses. The positioning is: "a team you hire," not "a platform you use."

## The Bridge Pitch

One paragraph that works for both audiences:

> "YARNNN is an AI team you hire to run your content product business. The team — Researcher, Analyst, Writer, Tracker, Designer, orchestrated by a Thinking Partner — accumulates domain expertise in your niche and produces content products on schedule. You direct and review, 30 minutes a week. They handle everything else. The team gets better every cycle — research gets more targeted, production aligns to your standards, analytics get more useful. After 6 months, the accumulated team intelligence is irreplaceable. Switch to any other tool and your next issue starts from zero. Your revenue proves the difference."

Contains:
- The team metaphor ("hire a team")
- The roles (concrete, human-equivalent)
- The editorial director model ("direct and review, 30 min/week")
- The compounding story ("team gets better every cycle")
- The moat ("irreplaceable, starts from zero")
- The measurement ("revenue proves the difference")
- The competitive position (implicit: no "platform" to compare against)
