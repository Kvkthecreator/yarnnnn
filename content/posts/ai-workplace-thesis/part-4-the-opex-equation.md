# SCAFFOLD — Part 4: The OpEx Equation

## Opening Hook
Here's a number that should make every CEO uncomfortable: Midjourney generates roughly $4.1 million in revenue per employee. Anysphere (Cursor) does $3.3 million. The traditional SaaS gold standard — the number that made investors excited — was $300,000.

That's not a 10% improvement. It's a 10x structural shift. And it has implications for every line item on an operating budget.

---

## Section 1: The Revenue Per Employee Revolution

**Key angle**: AI-native companies don't just do things faster — they operate on a fundamentally different economic model.

**Data points to use**:
- Midjourney: ~$200M revenue, <50 employees → $4.1M RPE
- Anysphere/Cursor: ~$100M ARR, 30 employees → $3.3M RPE
- OpenAI: $6.5B revenue, ~4,000 employees → $1.5M RPE (even at scale)
- Traditional SaaS gold standard: $300K RPE
- AI companies grow 4X faster with 7-8X fewer employees per dollar of revenue (Pavilion research)
- PwC: AI-exposed industries see labor productivity growing 4.8x faster than non-AI sectors

**Argument**: This isn't about replacing people with software. It's about a new kind of company where compute does the execution work and humans do the judgment work — and the cost structure reflects that shift. The org chart isn't just flatter. It's a different shape entirely.

---

## Section 2: The New Cost Categories

**Key angle**: Traditional OpEx categories (HR, office, travel, software) don't capture what AI-native companies actually spend money on.

**Kevin's framework to develop**:

### 2a. Token Utilization as OpEx
- AI-native companies don't pay humans to execute — they pay for compute tokens
- Token cost per output = the new unit economics
- Average org spending $85,521/month on AI-native apps (2025), 36% increase YoY
- This isn't "software cost" in the traditional sense — it's closer to "labor cost" because it replaces human execution hours
- The question for CFOs: What percentage of your OpEx is human labor vs. token labor? And how is that ratio shifting?

### 2b. HR Costs as % of Revenue
- Traditional company: HR costs (salaries, benefits, overhead) = 60-80% of OpEx
- AI-native company: HR costs can be 20-30% of OpEx, with token/compute costs filling the gap
- This doesn't mean fewer humans → it means humans concentrated in high-judgment roles, with corresponding higher per-person compensation
- Kevin's point: "the cost of an agent running on tokens is fractional compared to a salaried human doing the same execution work"

### 2c. Revenue Per Agent (New Metric)
- **Kevin's key insight**: Revenue per headcount (RPH) is only half the picture. The emerging metric is Revenue Per Agent (RPA) — how much value does each deployed AI agent generate?
- An employee who builds and manages 10 agents that each generate $50K in value annually is worth more than their salary suggests — they're an infrastructure builder, not a task executor
- This connects to Part 3's "builder premium" — incentivize employees to create agents because each agent compounds the org's leverage

## Section 3: The Org Chart Inversion

**Key angle**: AI doesn't just flatten the org chart — it inverts the traditional pyramid.

**Points to develop**:
- Traditional org: Many execution workers → some managers → few executives
- AI-native org: Few architects/builders → AI execution layer → thin coordination layer
- **Middle management**: Part 1 flagged this as "either the most automated role or the most important one." Part 4 follows the money: if middle managers primarily coordinate information flow, and AI handles information flow, the cost of that layer becomes hard to justify
- **BUT**: Someone has to orchestrate the human-AI workflow. The surviving management role is "AI operations" — deciding which processes get agent-ified, monitoring quality, handling exceptions
- **Kevin's org vision**: Not a pyramid, not even a flat structure — a network. Humans as nodes connected through agents. Each human manages a portfolio of AI agents rather than a team of humans.

**Data points**:
- Gartner: 40% of enterprise apps will embed AI agents by end of 2026
- Agentic AI market: $5.25B (2024) → $199B (2034), 43.84% CAGR
- 79% of organizations report some level of agentic AI adoption

## Section 4: The Multi-Variant Operating Model

**Key angle**: The AI-native company runs on multiple interconnected variables, not a single P&L structure.

**Kevin's framework to develop**:
- Traditional operating model: Fixed headcount × fixed hours × variable output = revenue
- AI-native operating model: Variable headcount × variable hours × AI-augmented output + agent output = revenue
- **Four variables that create a synergistic org**:
  1. **Work**: What gets produced (human-judged, AI-executed)
  2. **Time**: How hours are allocated (flexible, optionality-based from Part 2)
  3. **Performance**: Quality of judgment, not volume (from Part 3)
  4. **Cost**: Blended human + token economics
- This creates a new kind of financial model where you can optimize for different outcomes: maximum revenue (sprint mode), maximum sustainability (balance mode), maximum innovation (R&D mode)

---

## Section 5: The Infrastructure Investment Thesis

**Key angle**: Smart AI-native companies invest in agent infrastructure, not headcount.

**Points to develop**:
- Hiring a person = recurring cost with linear output capacity
- Building an agent = upfront investment with compounding output capacity
- The employee who builds agents is more valuable than the employee who does the work the agent could do — connect to Part 3's builder premium
- **Kevin's point**: "Incentivize agent building. Don't dis-incentivize it." The biggest risk is companies where employees avoid automation because it threatens their role.
- Capital allocation shifts: Less on recruiting, more on compute. Less on training humans for execution tasks, more on training humans for judgment + orchestration.

## Closing

**The uncomfortable math**: AI-native companies can generate 10x the revenue per employee. This means either the workforce gets 10x smaller for the same revenue, or the same workforce generates 10x the value. The choice isn't economic — it's ethical. And that's where Part 5 picks up.

**Connect forward**: Part 4 followed the money. Part 5 follows the consequences — what happens when agent-to-agent ecosystems emerge, financial outcomes become radically lopsided, and companies have to decide what kind of organization they actually want to be.
