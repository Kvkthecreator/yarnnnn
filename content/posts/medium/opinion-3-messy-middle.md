The AI conversation right now is polarized in a way that's actively unhelpful. On one side: AI is a tool, use it like you use Excel, nothing fundamentally changes. On the other side: AI replaces everything, white-collar work collapses by 2028, start hoarding canned goods.

I think both camps are wrong — not because the truth is somewhere in the middle (that's lazy), but because they're both skipping an entire era of work that we'll actually live in for the next decade.

## Three Eras, One Transition Nobody's Building For

**Era 1: Human-Driven Work.** AI as autocomplete. You prompt ChatGPT, it responds, you copy-paste the useful parts into your actual work. This is where most knowledge workers still are in early 2026.

**Era 2: Human + AI (Supervised).** AI handles execution — drafting, research, synthesis, monitoring — while humans handle judgment. The AI gets better at execution over time because it accumulates context about your specific work. This is the messy middle.

**Era 3: Agent-to-Agent.** Fully autonomous agents negotiating with each other. Your AI talks to your client's AI. Humans as stakeholders, not operators. This is where the hype points.

The discourse is stuck debating Era 1 versus Era 3. Almost nobody is seriously building for Era 2 — the era we're actually entering.

## The Cracks Are Real. The Timeline Is Not.

I'm not going to pretend the transition isn't happening. Claude Code is already reshaping how SaaS companies get valued. The cracks in the existing knowledge work economy are visible.

But cracks don't tell you the shape of the break, and they definitely don't tell you the timing. Existing data moats don't evaporate overnight. Switching costs are real. Regulatory posture varies wildly by geography.

## What Adoption Curve Are We Even On?

> This is the question I keep coming back to, and I don't think anyone has a good answer.

**Are we on the electricity curve?** The grid existed for 40 years before factories were actually redesigned around it. The first generation just replaced steam engines with electric motors in the same layout. The real revolution came a generation later.

**Are we on the internet curve?** Massive hype, spectacular crash, then the actual transformation happened quietly over the following decade. The winners weren't the ones dominating the conversation in 1999.

**Are we on the smartphone curve?** The device was ready before the ecosystem was. The app economy took five years after the iPhone launched.

**Or the containerization curve?** Malcom McLean's shipping container was technically simple. But the logistics system took 20 years to reorganize around a metal box.

I genuinely don't know which curve we're on. Trying to predict the timing is dangerous. But not thinking about it is worse.

## Agent-to-Agent: The Era Everyone Assumes and Nobody's Building the Rails For

Agent-to-Agent work requires an entirely new economic infrastructure that doesn't exist yet.

**An agent economy.** How do agents transact with each other? Today's payment rails are built for humans.

**Trust and verification.** When two agents negotiate an outcome, who audits the decision chain?

**Governance.** Who's liable when an agent-to-agent workflow produces a bad outcome?

**Regulatory rails.** GAAP, HIPAA, SOC 2 — none of these contemplate autonomous agent actors.

These aren't edge cases. They're the foundation. And none of it is built.

## The Question That Matters

> Are we so focused on making agents that can do the work that we've completely ignored building the world those agents would actually operate in?

Is that infrastructure — not intelligence — the real bottleneck?

I don't have the answer. But I think it's the question that matters most right now, and almost nobody in the AI conversation is asking it.
