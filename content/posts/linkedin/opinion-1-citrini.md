# Citrini's 2028 Crisis: A Builder's Response to AI Doomerism

Citrini Research's "2028 Global Intelligence Crisis" spooked markets, tanked IBM 13%, and got amplified by Michael Burry. Sixteen million views. The thesis: AI creates "Ghost GDP," displaces white-collar workers en masse, triggers a deflationary spiral by 2028.

I read it the week it dropped. As someone who ships AI agents daily — not models, not research, actual products that do work for real users — my reaction was: this is a brilliant thought experiment written by someone who has never tried to get an AI agent to reliably do a Tuesday.

## The feedback loop has friction everywhere

Citrini assumes AI capabilities translate instantly to production deployment. They don't.

The real loop: AI improves → companies try to deploy it → tool errors, hallucination rates, context window limits, integration complexity → months of iteration → partial deployment → some roles restructured → some new roles created → meanwhile, AI improved again and the integration work partially resets.

The gap between "AI can do this in a demo" and "AI reliably does this in production" is the 80% problem. AI gets 80% right, the last 20% takes longer than doing the whole thing manually. Every builder I know lives in this gap.

## AI intensifies work. It doesn't eliminate it.

Harvard Business Review published a study in February 2026 — "AI Doesn't Reduce Work — It Intensifies It." Eight months observing workers at a tech company. The finding: employees who used AI worked faster, took on broader tasks, extended their hours. 62% of entry-level workers reported burnout — not from displacement, but from doing more.

This is the opposite of Citrini's thesis. AI isn't making workers unnecessary. It's making workers do more.

## What I'm actually seeing

I build YARNNN — a platform where AI agents handle recurring knowledge work. These are exactly the tasks Citrini assumes get automated away.

Nobody is firing anyone. People are serving more clients with the same team. They're handling deliverables that weren't economically viable before. The consultant who uses AI to produce 3x the output doesn't vanish from the economy — they grow their practice.

This is the Jevons paradox applied to knowledge work. Making intelligence cheaper doesn't reduce demand for it. It increases the surface area of what becomes worth doing.

## What Citrini gets right

The repricing of white-collar work is happening. The transition will be disorderly. Institutions aren't ready. But the disorder isn't a death spiral — it's the messy middle, the era where humans and AI figure out how to work together.

Citrini's scenario assumes we jump from "AI is a tool" straight to "AI replaces everything" overnight. That skip — over the decade where humans and AI actually learn to coexist — is where the analysis breaks down.

The question isn't whether AI will change work. It already is. The question is whether we build the products, institutions, and trust frameworks that make the change productive rather than destructive.
