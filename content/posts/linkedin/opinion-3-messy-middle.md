# The Messy Middle of AI Work: Why the Future Isn't Tools or Full Autonomy

The AI conversation is polarized. One side: AI is a tool, nothing fundamentally changes. Other side: AI replaces everything by 2028. Both camps are wrong — not because the truth is in the middle, but because they're both skipping an entire era of work we'll actually live in for the next decade.

## Three eras

Era 1: Human-Driven. AI as autocomplete. You prompt, you do the work. Where most people still are.

Era 2: Human + AI (Supervised). AI handles execution — drafting, research, synthesis. Humans handle judgment — what to produce, whether it's good enough. The messy middle.

Era 3: Agent-to-Agent. Fully autonomous agents negotiating with each other. Where the hype points.

The discourse is stuck debating Era 1 vs Era 3. Almost nobody is building for Era 2 — the era we're entering.

## The cracks are real. The timeline is not.

Claude Code is reshaping how SaaS companies get valued. The repricing is happening. But cracks don't tell you the shape of the break.

Data moats don't evaporate overnight. Switching costs are real. Regulatory posture varies wildly by geography. The EU, US, Japan, Korea — each adapting at different speeds.

## What adoption curve are we even on?

Electricity took 40 years before factories redesigned around it. The internet had a spectacular crash before the real transformation happened quietly. Smartphones launched years before the app economy mattered. Containerization — a simple metal box — took 20 years to reorganize global logistics.

I don't know which curve we're on. I suspect it's a blend — fast in some sectors, slow in others, unpredictable where regulatory and social friction create bottlenecks nobody modeled.

## The A2A gap nobody's talking about

Agent-to-Agent work requires infrastructure that doesn't exist yet.

An agent economy — how do agents transact with each other? Trust and verification — who audits agent-to-agent decisions? Governance — who's liable when an autonomous workflow goes wrong? Regulatory rails — GAAP, HIPAA, SOC 2 don't contemplate agent actors.

These aren't edge cases. They're the foundation. None of it is built.

The question I can't stop asking: are we so focused on making agents that can do the work that we've completely ignored building the world those agents would operate in?

Is that infrastructure — not intelligence — the real bottleneck?

I don't have the answer. But I think it's the question that matters most right now.
