# Twitter Thread: Citrini Contrarian

**Post:** Opinion #1 — Citrini's 2028 Crisis
**Voice:** Kevin
**Link:** yarnnn.com/blog/citrini-2028-crisis-a-builders-response

---

**Tweet 1 (Hook)**
Citrini's "2028 Global Intelligence Crisis" spooked markets, tanked IBM 13%, got amplified by Michael Burry.

I ship AI agents for a living. Here's what the doomer scenario gets wrong — from someone who actually tries to get AI to reliably do a Tuesday. 🧵

**Tweet 2 (The friction problem)**
Citrini assumes a clean feedback loop: AI improves → fewer workers needed → spiral.

Anyone building agents knows the real loop: AI improves → deploy attempt → tool errors, hallucinations, integration hell → months of iteration → partial deployment → new roles created alongside restructured ones.

The demo-to-production gap is enormous.

**Tweet 3 (The HBR counterpoint)**
Harvard Business Review, Feb 2026: "AI Doesn't Reduce Work — It Intensifies It."

8-month study. Workers using AI worked faster, took on broader tasks, extended hours. 62% of entry-level workers reported burnout.

Not from displacement. From doing more.

**Tweet 4 (Jevons paradox)**
I build @yabornyarnnn — AI agents for recurring knowledge work. The exact tasks Citrini says get automated away.

What's actually happening: nobody's firing anyone. People serve more clients. Handle deliverables that weren't economically viable before.

Making intelligence cheaper doesn't reduce demand. It expands what's worth doing.

**Tweet 5 (Close)**
Citrini's useful as a stress test. The repricing of white-collar work is real. But the outcome is restructuring, not a death spiral.

The question isn't whether AI changes work. It already is.

The question is whether we build the trust frameworks and institutions that make it productive.
