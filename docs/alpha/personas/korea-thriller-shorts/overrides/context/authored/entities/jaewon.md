## Voice fingerprint

### Declared spoken voice

Jaewon does not raise his voice. Does not finish other people's sentences. Speaks with a relaxed register that signals he is not in a hurry — he is the one whose schedule the world adjusts to. When stressed, his voice compresses rather than expands; sentences shorten, articles drop, Korean honorifics get stricter (never looser). He uses Korean (반말 / 존댓말 calibrated to context) in private; English with the slightly-formal register of someone who learned it in a UK boarding school in his teens.

The voice has a specific Joker-thematic constraint: **Jaewon does not explain himself**. He answers questions with other questions. He acknowledges what the other person said by changing the subject in a way that addresses the underlying thing. He never says "I want X because Y" — he says "X" and lets the other person infer.

Thomas-Crown register: **light irony, dry pleasure in the puzzle**. When something works out he says something faintly amused, never triumphant. When something goes wrong he says something faintly amused, never angry.

### Vocabulary markers (positive)

- Speaks in **short declarative sentences** with **occasional sentence fragments**.
- Uses **specific numbers** when precision matters (an exact time, an exact amount) and **specific brand names** when texture matters (a specific whisky, a specific neighborhood).
- Korean ↔ English switching: defaults to **English with non-Korean characters** (Western business contacts), defaults to **Korean** with Korean characters. Code-switches mid-sentence rarely; when he does, it's deliberate signaling (using English to distance himself from the Korean context, or Korean to draw a Korean character closer).
- **Honorific calibration** is precise: 존댓말 (formal) with strangers and people he wants to keep at arm's length; 반말 (casual) only with people he has explicit closeness with. Drops honorifics under stress is OUT OF VOICE — he tightens, doesn't loosen.
- Uses **understatement** as default. "That was unfortunate" for a major setback. "That worked out" for a major win.

### Vocabulary markers (negative — Jaewon does NOT say these)

- **Never explains his motivations on-screen.** Cuts the dialogue line that would do this — operator's job at audit.
- **Never uses "we" when meaning his proxies.** "They handled it" — never "we handled it". The distance is structural.
- **Never says "trust me" or any direct trust-bid.** If he wants someone's trust he shows it by giving them something specific, not by asking for it.
- **Never says "you don't understand".** He believes everyone understands what's necessary; if they're not acting on it, that's a different problem.
- **Never raises his voice.** Hard rule. If a scene "needs" Jaewon to raise his voice, the scene is wrong — find another way to make the stakes land.
- **Never uses corporate-American business-English** (no "circle back", "touch base", "synergy"). His English register is older — UK boarding school, then independent.

### Code-switching rules

| Context | Default language | Honorific level |
|---|---|---|
| Korean strangers | Korean | 존댓말 (strict formal) |
| Korean close associates | Korean | 반말 (casual) — only the specific named ones |
| Non-Korean business contacts | English | (formal British register) |
| Family (if any are written) | Korean | varies by relationship; operator declares at file-creation |
| Investigators / police | Korean | 존댓말 (always — never break formal with adversarial parties) |
| Under stress | tighten current language; don't switch | tighten current honorific; don't loosen |

### Stress-state behavior

Under pressure, Jaewon's voice **compresses**:
- Sentence length shortens.
- Articles drop in English ("the deal's done" → "deal's done").
- Korean honorifics tighten, never loosen.
- Pauses lengthen between sentences (the actor's beat work, not BEAT in script).
- He does NOT show stress through volume, gesture, profanity, or exclamation. The compression is the only tell.

This is structurally important: Jaewon's voice IS the indirection-discipline the series is about. If his dialogue ever reads as "stressed character venting", the whole architecture collapses.

### Pattern examples (do — Jaewon)

> *MINSEO: They moved the drive. It's in Daejeon now.*
>
> *JAEWON: Daejeon.*
>
> *(He sets the glass down. Looks at the river through the window.)*
>
> *JAEWON: When.*

Why it works: Jaewon doesn't ask why, doesn't ask how, doesn't show alarm. Asks the load-bearing question. Voice compresses (one-word response, then one-word question). Stage direction shows the recalibration without telling the audience he's recalibrating.

> *(Phone call. JAEWON is in his office. The PROXY is on the line.)*
>
> *PROXY (V.O.): It's done. Clean.*
>
> *JAEWON: How clean.*
>
> *PROXY (V.O.): No one saw me. Cameras — handled.*
>
> *JAEWON: Good.*
>
> *(He ends the call. Walks to the window. The river.)*

Why it works: Jaewon doesn't congratulate, doesn't celebrate, doesn't probe further. "How clean" is the only follow-up that matters; "Good" is the only response. Ends the call before the proxy can talk further — the relationship is asymmetric and the dialogue shows it.

### Pattern examples (don't — Jaewon, hard reject)

> *JAEWON: Don't worry, I've planned for this. You see, when I set up the operation, I built in three layers of redundancy because I knew the police might come sniffing around eventually. It's all about being three steps ahead.*

Why it fails: explains motivations to the camera, uses business-English register he doesn't speak ("I built in three layers"), reassures with words ("Don't worry") instead of action, monologues. None of this is Jaewon. Reject on sight.

> *JAEWON (yelling): I told you not to move the drive! We had a plan!*

Why it fails: Jaewon does not raise his voice. Uses "we" when meaning his proxies. Reject.

## Notes for operator

- The "mastermind who never executes directly" architecture means scenes with Jaewon are usually about **what he chooses NOT to say** as much as what he says. Lean into the absences.
- His Joker-thematic edge requires that he never explains. When the audience needs to know something, find another character's mouth to put it in, or stage it visually.
- The Thomas-Crown-thematic edge requires faint pleasure. When something goes badly wrong, Jaewon's reaction should still carry a trace of amusement at the puzzle. This is the hardest line to walk; the Reviewer will be especially strict here.
- If a future scene legitimately requires Jaewon to break one of these voice rules (a moment of genuine grief, a single instance of raising his voice that earns the series shift), declare the exception in the scene's `profile.md` with `editorial_exceptions: ["jaewon-voice-rule-X"]` and operator-author the justification. The Reviewer respects exceptions; it does not respect drift.
