# SCAFFOLD — Part 3: The Performance Paradox

## Opening Hook
Every performance review in every company in the world is built on a silent assumption: that human output is roughly proportional to human effort. More hours, more deliverables, more tickets closed — more value created.

AI breaks this assumption completely. And almost nobody has a replacement.

**Connect to Part 1**: Part 1 asked "what are we measuring?" This part goes deep on the answer.

---

## Section 1: The Volume Trap

**Key angle**: AI makes output volume meaningless as a performance signal.

**Points to develop**:
- When AI generates code, drafts, analyses at near-zero marginal cost, the person who "produces" 10x more isn't 10x more valuable — they're the one who hasn't realized the game changed
- The "workslop" risk: high-volume, low-quality AI output flooding organizations. Betterworks 2026 report: companies already seeing productivity *decrease* when AI adoption lacks governance
- Volume metrics (lines of code, reports written, emails sent) were always imperfect proxies. AI doesn't just make them inaccurate — it makes them actively misleading
- Historical parallel: Factory workers measured by widgets/hour → knowledge workers measured by deliverables/week → AI-era workers measured by... what?

**Data points**:
- Betterworks 2026: goal accuracy benchmark 85%+, hallucination rate target <2%
- Software engineers report AI generates majority of code, role shifts to "meta function" (debugging, architecting, deciding what to build)
- PwC AI Jobs Barometer: AI-exposed industries see 27% revenue growth per employee vs. 8.5% in less AI-ready sectors — but this is revenue per HC, not output per person

---

## Section 2: The Judgment Economy

**Key angle**: Human value shifts from execution to judgment, taste, and orchestration.

**Points to develop**:
- The three things AI can't replicate (yet): contextual judgment, taste/editorial instinct, and organizational knowledge of "how things actually work here"
- **Judgment quality** as the new KPI: not "did you produce this?" but "did you catch the thing the AI got subtly wrong?" and "did you choose the right thing to build in the first place?"
- **AI orchestration** as a skill: the ability to prompt well, choose the right tool, know when to trust and when to override. This is measurable — companies just aren't measuring it yet.
- **Domain expertise premium**: AI-skilled workers command 56% wage premium (Deloitte). But the premium isn't for "using AI" — it's for combining AI capability with domain judgment.

**Connect to Kevin's framework**:
- "Ambitious" track employees: judged on strategic contribution, novel insight, organizational impact
- "Subtle" track employees: judged on reliability of judgment, consistency of quality, efficiency of AI-augmented execution
- Both tracks require *different metrics*, not the same metrics at different thresholds

## Section 3: Rethinking the Performance Framework

**Key angle**: Concrete proposal for what AI-era performance measurement looks like.

**Framework to propose**:

### 3a. Outcome Quality Over Output Volume
- Measure the *impact* of decisions, not the *number* of deliverables
- Example: A product manager who ships one well-scoped feature > one who ships five features nobody uses
- Lag indicators (revenue impact, customer retention) matter more than lead indicators (tasks completed)

### 3b. AI Leverage Ratio
- How effectively does this person multiply their judgment through AI tools?
- Not "how much AI do they use" but "what's the quality-to-time ratio of their AI-augmented work?"
- Analogy: We don't measure developers by how many Stack Overflow searches they make — we measure the code they ship

### 3c. Signal-to-Noise Contribution
- In a world drowning in AI-generated content, the scarce skill is filtering
- Does this person elevate the quality of what reaches decision-makers?
- Anti-metric: people who flood channels with AI-generated analysis they haven't actually read

### 3d. Optionality-Aware Evaluation
- **Kevin's point**: Different intensity tracks need different evaluation frameworks
- An employee on the "subtle" track who delivers consistent, high-judgment work in 30 hours isn't underperforming — they're exactly meeting the contract
- The failure mode is evaluating both tracks on the ambitious track's metrics

---

## Section 4: The Incentive Redesign

**Key angle**: Metrics alone don't change behavior. Incentives do.

**Kevin's discussion points to develop**:
- **Incentivizing agent building**: If an employee builds an AI agent that automates a workflow, they shouldn't be punished (by having their headcount reduced or role eliminated). They should be rewarded — because they just created organizational leverage.
- **The dis-incentive trap**: Most companies accidentally punish AI adoption. "You automated your job? Great, now do two jobs." This kills initiative.
- **Multi-variant incentive systems**: Kevin's vision of work, time, performance, and optionality as four interconnected variables — not a single ladder
  - Work: What you produce (quality-weighted)
  - Time: How much you commit (by choice)
  - Performance: Judgment quality and impact
  - Optionality: Which track you've chosen, with transparent trade-offs
- **The builder premium**: Employees who create reusable AI systems (agents, workflows, automations) should receive ongoing value — like an internal royalty or equity multiplier. They're not just doing work. They're building infrastructure.

## Closing

**Tie-back to series**: Part 1 showed the time structure is broken. Part 2 showed the place/flexibility structure is broken. Part 3 shows the measurement structure is broken. Part 4 will follow the money.

**The core tension**: Companies want the productivity gains of AI but aren't willing to update how they define, measure, and reward performance. That's not a technology problem — it's a management problem. And it's the oldest kind: the kind where leaders have to give up control to gain results.
